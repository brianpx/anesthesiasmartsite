Clinical AI Decision Card

Verify → Decide → Document · Bedside Quick Reference

① Verify

Does the signal match independent clinical assessment?

✓ Is the signal plausible given patient context?
✓ Could this be artifact or equipment error?
✓ What does direct patient assessment show?
✓ Can I trace the data source?

② Decide

AI informs. You own the judgment boundary.

✓ Patient history and comorbidities
✓ Balance of competing harms
✓ Reversibility of proposed action
✓ Surgical team communication

③ Document

Chart your reasoning, not the algorithm.

✓ Name the alert or tool evaluated
✓ Record your independent assessment
✓ State your decision with rationale
✓ Never write "AI recommended; agreed"

Documentation Template

"[Tool name] alert evaluated. [Clinical finding]. [Relevant vitals/context]. [Conclusion: artifact suspected / clinically consistent / etc.]. [Action or no action and reason]."

Example:

"HPI alert 87 evaluated. A-line waveform damped. MAP 68, stable. Artifact suspected. No vasopressor at this time. Will reassess in 5 minutes."

AI reasons. Humans judge. Verify. Decide. Document.

Two Categories of AI

Understanding the distinction between cognitive and clinical AI tools

Cognitive AI

Summarizes · Explains · Drafts · Compares

• ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini (consumer platforms)
• Use to think, brainstorm, and explore concepts
• Never for patient-specific clinical decisions
• No PHI outside approved, governed pathways
Rule: Use it to think. Never use it to decide.

Clinical AI

Detects · Predicts · Overlays · Flags

• HPI, echo overlay, ambient documentation
• FDA-cleared, enterprise-deployed systems
• Requires VDD supervision with every use
• Prediction is not permission to treat
Rule: AI informs. You judge. Always document.

Five Pre-Deployment Questions

Essential validation before any AI tool goes live

Before any clinical AI deployment, these five questions must be answered clearly:

Does it represent my patients? Was the tool trained and validated on data that reflects your actual patient population, case mix, and clinical workflows?
Was it validated here? Local pilot with real cases in your environment — not just vendor slides or published papers from other institutions.
Can I defend "follow" or "override"? You must understand the tool's failure modes well enough to justify both agreement and disagreement with its output.
Who owns failure? When the system is wrong, who is accountable? Not in theory — in practice. A named Clinical Owner must be assigned before deployment.
Who owns the kill switch? If the tool starts causing harm or over-alerting, who has the authority to suspend it immediately without committee approval?
If these five questions cannot be answered clearly — that is a hard stop, not a soft concern.

Department Governance Readiness

Pre-deployment gap analysis to prevent another Sedasys failure

A. CLINICAL OWNERSHIP

A named physician is designated as Clinical Owner for this tool
Clinical Owner has reviewed the validation study and understands failure modes
Clinical Owner has authority to suspend the tool without committee approval
Backup Clinical Owner is named for coverage during absence

B. KILL SWITCH & ESCALATION

Suspension protocol exists: who initiates, how, and in what timeframe
Kill switch authority is documented and known to frontline staff
Escalation path is defined: clinical owner → department chief → risk management
Post-suspension review process is defined

C. LOCAL VALIDATION

Tool was validated in a local pilot before full deployment
Pilot cohort reflected actual case mix and patient population
Performance metrics were measured in local workflow
Alert threshold was calibrated to local false-positive/false-negative tolerance

D. DOCUMENTATION STANDARD

Documentation template exists for this specific tool
Template shared with all clinical staff who use this tool
Template captures: signal acknowledged, independent assessment, decision rationale
Prohibited phrase "AI recommended; agreed" is flagged in onboarding

E. ONGOING MONITORING

Metric set established before go-live (override rate, alert burden, adverse events)
Baseline measurement recorded at launch
Review cadence scheduled (minimum: quarterly)
Alert performance reviewed and compared to baseline

Calibration vs. Discrimination

Understanding what AI performance metrics actually tell you

Vendors present AUROC. Patients need calibration. These are different questions — and confusing them is one of the most common errors in clinical AI evaluation.

Discrimination (AUROC)

The question it answers:

"Can the model rank high-risk patients above low-risk ones?"

How it's reported:

AUROC (Area Under ROC), also called AUC or c-statistic. Ranges from 0.5 (coin flip) to 1.0 (perfect).

What high AUROC tells you:

The model separates high-risk from low-risk patients in the test dataset. Says nothing about whether predicted probabilities are accurate.

Clinical reality:

A model correctly ranking a sick patient above a healthy one has good discrimination — even if it predicts 99% risk when true risk is 60%.

Calibration

The question it answers:

"When the model says 80% probability, does the event actually happen 80% of the time — in my patients?"

How it's reported:

Calibration plots, Hosmer-Lemeshow test, Expected/Observed (E/O) ratio. Rarely shown in vendor presentations.

What good calibration tells you:

Predicted probabilities are trustworthy as absolute numbers. A 30% alert means something different than a 90% alert.

Clinical reality:

A weather app ranking rainy days above sunny ones but saying 90% rain when actual rate is 40% leaves you carrying an umbrella unnecessarily.

Three Critical Questions to Ask Every Vendor

Q1: "What is the calibration of this model in your external validation cohort, and do you have a calibration plot we can review?"

Red flag: Vendor only shows AUROC and cannot produce a calibration plot.

Q2: "Was this model validated on a population similar to ours — in terms of age, ASA class, procedure type, and EMR platform?"

Red flag: Validation cohort is academic, predominantly young, or from a single institution with different workflows.

Q3: "When was the model last recalibrated? Do you offer local recalibration, and what does that process look like?"

Red flag: Vendor cannot define a recalibration schedule or says it is not needed post-deployment.

High ROC ≠ Local Trustworthiness

How to Build a Department AI Guide

Strategic roadmap for institutional AI governance

A department-specific AI guide is a written strategic roadmap that answers three fundamental questions for your specialty:

What AI tools are relevant?

How do we evaluate tools for clinical fit, safety, and evidence quality?

How do we deploy them?

What governance structures ensure responsible implementation and ongoing monitoring?

How do we train people?

What competencies do clinicians need to safely supervise AI tools?

Why Your Department Needs One

✓ AI tools are already arriving — in vendor contracts, Epic updates, and residents' pockets, with or without department guidance
✓ Governance vacuums cause harm — Sedasys failed not from bad technology but from unclear ownership and no escalation path
✓ Clinicians need a framework — 76% of UC Davis staff wanted AI training; 50% had never used AI professionally
✓ External frameworks don't fit — NIST, AMA, FDA guidance is general. Your specialty's workflows and risks are specific

Essential Components

1. Needs Assessment: Survey current AI use, identify adoption gaps, document barriers by role
2. Task Force Composition: Named membership with defined roles, meeting cadence, methodology transparency
3. Governance Framework: Approved platforms, oversight committee, human-in-the-loop policy, PHI rules
4. Clinical Use Cases: Specialty-specific applications with supervision requirements
5. Training & Competency: Role-based education, verification methods, documentation standards
6. Ongoing Monitoring: Performance metrics, drift detection, periodic review schedule
7. Escalation Protocol: Clear ownership, suspension authority, post-incident review process
8. Annual Review: Update cycle, version control, stakeholder feedback mechanism
